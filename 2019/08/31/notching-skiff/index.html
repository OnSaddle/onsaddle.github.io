Notching Skiff to Retrieve Sword 刻舟求劍

Author: Wolf Date: August 31, 2019  16:06:24 Category: Yangtze

Once upon a time, a swordsman from the Chu state was traveling on the Yangtze in a skiff.

Yangtze could be gentle as a pond while at times fierce as the ocean. One day his sword fell overboard and disappeared into the muddy rapids.

To save his most treasured companion the swordsman swiftly notched the skiff with a knife to mark the position where the sword had fell. When he finally managed to stablize and stop the boat, he dived right at the position of the notch to salvage his sword.

His efforts were in vain after all. Yangtze had carried the skiff away since he carved the notch. The sword was now with Yangtze forever.

Notching Skiff to Retrieve Sword (刻舟求劍 kè zhōu qiú jiàn) is a well-known story from Master Lü’s Spring and Autumn Annals 呂氏春秋 . It has been so well-known over the centuries, that eventually it became an idiom in the Chinese language used to describe those ill-fated efforts by dim-witted people.

Parents and teachers have been teaching this idiom to the children for many, many generations.

But I think they missed the point.

What is lost is lost.

At some point in our life, things we treasured are bound to be lost, like being taken away by the Yangtze, and never to re-emerge.

Notching our skiff, in my opinion, is perhaps the best way to remember it.

Life is only meaningful thanks to memory. Through memory we travel upstream the river of time and relive the past, diving again and again into the moments that once shined vivid and bright.

In a sense, as long as we can remember them, they are not totally gone.

Memory is the ONLY means to retrieve the lost sword.

The nameless protagonist of this story, who notched his skiff and dived perversely, was a true swordsman.
